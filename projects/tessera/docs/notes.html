A Foundational Document

Notes on the Tessera

A writing system whose 104 characters are 3×3 binary pixel grids, organized by symmetry, decoded into a calligraphy. This document explains why, how, and what was decided along the way.

1 · Why a 3×3 grid?

The starting observation is small but provocative: a 3×3 binary grid has exactly 512 distinct fillings, but when we identify each filling with its rotations and reflections, only 102 remain. And 102 is — give or take — the size of a working alphabet. The printable ASCII range has 95 characters; a typeset Latin font with digits, punctuation and both cases of letters carries about 70. So the question presents itself: can we use the 102 orbits of a 3×3 grid as a writing system?

The answer, it turns out, is yes — with some surprising elegance. The orbits don't merely fit the size of an alphabet; their internal structure (orbit size, symmetry type, popcount) suggests which characters they should host. The singlets — orbits invariant under every symmetry of the square — naturally take the most universal symbols: the period, the space, the cardinal operators. The doublets become the numerals. The high-symmetry quadruplets host the vowels. And a small number-theoretic coincidence (call it the +centre rule) lets us encode long vowels and retroflex consonants as the simple operation of switching on the centre pixel.

What follows is the story of how the assignment came together, and the calligraphic rules that translate each pixel pattern into a single hand-written glyph.

2 · The symmetries of the square

A square admits eight rigid symmetries: the four rotations (by 0°, 90°, 180° and 270°) and four reflections (through the two edge bisectors and the two diagonals). These eight operations form a group called D₄, the dihedral group of the square. Every symmetry of the square is one of these eight; nothing else preserves both its outline and its corners.

When D₄ acts on the 512 possible pixel grids, two grids end up in the same orbit precisely when one can be carried to the other by one of these eight operations. Two grids that look the same up to rotation or reflection are treated as the same character — exactly as we treat p and q as different letters even though they are 180° rotations of each other (a writing system is free to break a symmetry; ours, deliberately, does not).

3 · Counting orbits — Burnside's lemma

To count orbits we use a classical tool from group theory: Burnside's lemma, which says that the number of orbits equals the average number of elements fixed by each group element. Or, equivalently:

|orbits| = (1/|G|) · Σ |Fix(g)| for g ∈ G

For each of the eight elements of D₄ we count how many of the 512 colourings it leaves unchanged:

Burnside's lemma gives us 816 ÷ 8 = 102 orbits.

Why this works A 90° rotation permutes the 9 cells in a particular way — the centre is fixed, and the eight outer cells fall into two 4-cycles (the four corners; the four edge-centres). A colouring is fixed by the rotation only if all cells in a cycle share the same colour: hence 3 independent binary choices, 2³ = 8 fixed colourings. Similar reasoning applies to the other symmetries.

4 · The orbit zoo

The 102 orbits are not all alike. By the orbit-stabilizer theorem, the size of an orbit divides 8 — so every orbit has size 1, 2, 4 or 8. How the 102 break down:

The structure suggests its own ordering. The eight singlets are the most symmetric patterns possible: they survive every rotation and every reflection. They are the "universally invariant" patterns of the system — naturally the seats of universally recognised marks like the period, the cardinal arithmetic operators, and the empty cell.

The eight doublets are slightly less symmetric: each is invariant under a four-element subgroup of D₄ (either a "Klein four-group" made of {identity, 180°, H, V} or {identity, 180°, ↘, ↗}). The two sub-groups give us two families of doublets — axis-symmetric and diagonal-symmetric — four each. They turn out to be exactly the right number, in exactly the right structure, to carry the digits 2–9.

5 · Chirality and the brackets

Among the quadruplets, two stand apart. Their stabilizer is the rotation subgroup C₄ — they survive all four rotations of the square, but no reflection takes any of their elements to itself. Reflection takes them to a mirror-image element of the orbit instead.

These are the chiral orbits: orbits that have "handedness", in which left and right are genuinely different. There are exactly two of them, one at popcount 4 and one at popcount 5.

This is precisely the structure we need for matched delimiters. A chiral orbit splits, under rotation alone, into two sub-orbits of two elements each — each pair being mirror images of the other pair. So each chiral orbit yields two characters: an "opening" and a "closing" version, visually distinct, visually paired. The popcount-4 orbit gives us the square brackets [ ]; the popcount-5 orbit gives us the parentheses ( ).

A consequence The chiral orbits are the only orbits in the system that supply two characters. Every other orbit — singlet, doublet, axial, diagonal, octuplet — hosts exactly one character whose calligraphic form is independent of any mirror flipping. The brackets are the only place in the alphabet where mirror image is a distinguishing feature.

6 · Assignment philosophy

With 102 orbits and approximately 100 characters of a working alphabet to assign, the question is: which character goes where? We adopted a single guiding principle: simpler symbols belong in higher-symmetry orbits. Universal, language-independent marks like period, space, plus and equals get the singlets — the most symmetric patterns. Digits get the doublets — slightly less symmetric, but more internally-structured than singletons. Letters fill the quadruplets and octuplets, with vowels (the simplest sounds) preferring the higher-symmetry quadruplets and consonants taking the rest.

This is not a derivation, but an aesthetic. It produces, however, several emergent regularities — the +centre rule, the matching of even/odd digits to axis/diagonal symmetry, the relationship between dental and retroflex consonants — that suggest the aesthetic is doing more than just looking tidy.

7 · The eight singlets become operators

The pattern is striking: the singlets are exactly the patterns built from "orbit-cycle equivalence classes" — fill in some combination of {none of the nine pixels}, {just the centre}, {just the four edge-centres}, {just the four corners}, and {centre + four corners}, etc. These are the only patterns invariant under all eight symmetries.

Their assignments are guided by visual analogy where one exists: FRAME (the closed border) becomes 0, the digit that looks like a closed loop. POINT (just the centre) becomes 1, the smallest filled character. CRUX (a centred cross) becomes +. ASTERISM (corners plus centre) becomes ×. SALTIRE (just the corners) becomes =. CARDINAL (just the edge-centres) becomes −. SOLID (all nine filled) becomes the period — the densest possible mark for the simplest of punctuation. And VOID (no pixels) becomes the space character.

8 · The eight doublets become digits

The eight doublets come in two families: four invariant under axis reflections (H, V), four invariant under diagonal reflections (↘, ↗). Within each family, popcounts come in pairs that sum to 9 (so 2 + 7, 3 + 6, etc.). The combinatorial structure suggests an assignment to the digits 2 – 9.

The digit pair (2, 3) at popcount 2; (4, 5) at popcount 3; (6, 7) at popcount 6; (8, 9) at popcount 7. The pattern feels right: every even digit is invariant under horizontal/vertical flips; every odd digit under diagonal flips.

The popcount-7 axis doublet — digit 8, two outer rails plus a centre cross — has been given the entirely undignified working name TIE FIGHTER, for reasons that will be immediately apparent to anyone who has seen it.

9 · Vowels and the +centre rule

Vowels live in the axis-symmetric and diagonal-symmetric quadruplets. Some were placed by visual analogy (e = TOP BAR is one horizontal stroke across the top, evocative of the bar over a long vowel; i = GOAL POSTS is two parallel verticals; u = ARCH is an inverted bowl). But the deeper structural feature is the +centre rule:

canonical(long vowel) = canonical(short vowel) + bit 4

Switching on the centre pixel turns a short vowel into its long form, every time. This is not an accident: we chose vowel assignments specifically to make it hold. The five short/long pairs:

10 · Consonants and the retroflex pair

The consonants spread across the remaining quadruplets and most of the octuplets. A subset of them — the dentals t, d, n, s, l, z, r — have natural retroflex pairs in Indic and Dravidian phonology. We discovered, while assigning them, that the +centre rule extends to the dental/retroflex relationship for every one of these pairs.

canonical(retroflex) = canonical(dental) + bit 4

The centre pixel, in this case, is naturally read as a kind of tongue curl — a deepening or intensifying of the dental into its retroflex counterpart. Symbolically: the centre is intensification, length, depth. That this works simultaneously for vowel length and consonant retroflexion is the most pleasing structural surprise of the system.

11 · The Euro pack

After the core ASCII-equivalent inventory is laid down, we are left with exactly enough free orbits to host a working set of Central/Eastern European diacritics: á é í ó ú à è ì ò ù ý ö ü for accented vowels, and ř ś ź ń č ć ď đ ł ť for the carons, acutes and strokes used in Czech, Slovak, Polish and friends. Three of the free diagonal quadruplets (HARPOON, CASCADE, SHIFT) and one axial (TRIDENT) fill out the consonants; the rest go into octuplets.

The arithmetic is exact: 23 free orbits, 23 European characters. Whether this is mathematical providence or a coincidence noticed only because we went looking for it, we leave to the reader.

12 · From pixels to strokes — the calligraphic rules

A pixel pattern is not yet a writeable glyph. To take a 3×3 grid and produce something a human can hand-write requires a deterministic translation — a set of rules that turns "which pixels are filled" into "what strokes to make, in what order". We arrived at the following rule set after several iterations, each driven by a specific glyph that the previous version got wrong.

Rule 1
All nine pixels → a solid pressed block.
SOLID is the maximum-ink mark in the alphabet and serves as a period. Nothing else uses every cell.
Rule 2
Closed border (eight pixels, centre empty) → a single closed rectangle.
FRAME is the only orbit whose filled pixels form a single closed loop. Drawing the loop in one continuous motion is both faster and visually more recognisable than tracing the eight cells individually.
Rule 3
Filled 3 × 2 rectangle → two parallel lines.
UPPER BLOCK (the only such glyph in the system) is structurally two adjacent full rows. Drawing them as two parallel strokes — rather than as a 2×2 block plus an extra strip — preserves the parallel structure visually.
Rule 4
Full rows and columns fire as straight strokes — but only if at most one of their three pixels is already covered by a previous stroke.
This is the most consequential rule, and the one that took the most iteration. Without the overlap restriction, NAIL (two full rows plus a bottom pixel) would also fire the centre column, giving a tall vertical impaling both bars — a visually disastrous result. With the restriction, NAIL writes as two horizontal bars plus a short vertical stem below, which is correct.
Rule 5
A 2×2 block of uncovered pixels → a small oval.
The 2×2 block is the simplest "blob" — a four-pixel cluster reads naturally as a single round mark rather than as four separate strokes. Restricting to "uncovered" pixels ensures we do not absorb pixels that are already structural participants in a previous line stroke.
Rule 6
Diagonal triples fire as straight strokes, under the same at-most-one-overlap rule as full rows and columns.
A diagonal of three collinear pixels reads as one stroke; we give it the same overlap treatment as orthogonal full lines. ASTERISM (×) and DIAG. STROKE (5) and HARPOON all earn their diagonal strokes here.
Rule 7
Connector rule: any uncovered pixel orthogonally adjacent to a stroke pixel sprouts a short connector to it.
This rule is what makes ē a proper T (stem dropping from the top bar to the centre), ī a proper π (stems rising from the middle bar to the top corners), and u a proper arch. Without it, those pixels would appear as detached ticks floating beside their parent strokes.
Rule 8
Orthogonally adjacent uncovered pixels form a single continuous bent stroke. Diagonal adjacency never connects.
The "no diagonal adjacency" clause is critical: it is what cleanly distinguishes CORNER DOT (two diagonally-adjacent pixels: TL and centre) from DIAGONAL STROKE (three collinear pixels along the anti-diagonal). Under the rule, CORNER DOT becomes a TL tick plus a centre node — two clearly separated marks — while DIAGONAL STROKE becomes a single full anti-diagonal. No hand-writer can confuse the two.
Rule 9
Isolated pixels become directed ticks: a solid round head with a tangent-smooth arrowhead pointing toward the grid centre.
The tick design encodes the pixel's position: the arrowhead reveals which corner or edge of the grid the pixel lives in, while the round head is sized identically to the centre node — giving the whole alphabet a single coherent dot-vocabulary. Tangent construction (the straight edges of the arrowhead meet the circle without forming a corner) is what makes the marks read as one shape rather than two.
Rule 10
Whenever the centre pixel is filled, a solid centre node is overlaid on top of any strokes passing through it.
The centre node marks crossings explicitly. Because of the +centre rule, the centre is the most semantically loaded cell in the grid — its presence changes a short vowel to a long one, a dental to a retroflex — and the convention of always drawing a visible node when it is filled makes that significance immediately legible.

The order of operations

The rules are applied in the order listed. Rule 4 (orthogonal lines) runs before Rule 5 (2×2 blocks) so that two parallel full rows are recognised as such rather than absorbed into block + extra strip (NAIL, ḍ, ḷ all depend on this). Rule 6 (diagonal lines) runs after blocks so that 2×2 blocks reaching into a diagonal triple do not destabilise the block (this preserves TUMBLER, WEDGE, TOTEM and others as "circle plus tick" rather than "diagonal plus debris"). Rule 7 (connectors) runs after all lines so that bridges can attach to the lines they extend. Rule 8 (chains) handles whatever orthogonally-connected patches remain. Rule 9 (ticks) handles the rest. Rule 10 (centre node) is a final overlay.

13 · The centre pixel as orbit invariant

Several pairs of shapes look like mirrors of each other yet turn out to be in entirely different orbits. The archetype: f (g = 15, orbit J-TETRIMINO) and j (g = 57, orbit L-TETRIMINO). Both look like the J/L-tetrimino — three pixels in a row with one more at the end of the row. In Tetris the J and the L are mirror images; here they are not even that — they are separated by something stronger than chirality. Yet they cannot be the same orbit, and no rotation or reflection will ever carry one to the other.

The reason is that the centre pixel occupies the unique fixed point of D₄. Every element of the symmetry group — all four rotations and all four reflections — maps position (1, 1) to itself. It is the only cell with this property. As a consequence, "does this pattern have the centre pixel filled?" is an orbit invariant: if one member of an orbit has the centre filled, every member does, and no symmetry operation can ever change this.

The two populations Of the 102 orbits, exactly 49 have the centre pixel filled and 53 do not. These two populations never mix. Applying Burnside separately to the 256 centre-filled and 256 centre-empty grids confirms the 49 + 53 split.

For the alphabet this means:

f (g = 15, TL TM TR ML) has the centre empty. j (g = 57, TL ML C MR) has the centre filled. They look like two orientations of the same L-shape because they are — but one L lands its corner pixel on the top row (which never includes the centre) and the other lands it on the middle row (which always does). D₄ maps edge rows to edge rows and middle rows to middle rows; the two shapes are topologically separated. Similarly k (g = 29, centre filled) and x (g = 43, centre empty) share a visual family but cannot share an orbit.

The same invariant drives the +centre rule from section 9: adding bit 4 to a short vowel's g-value always produces the long vowel's g-value because they are in different orbits to begin with. The centre pixel does not "modify" a character; it selects an entirely different orbit that was waiting to be its partner.

The GoL ship being the digit 7 is especially satisfying. In Conway's Life it is one of the five 6-cell still-lifes; in this alphabet it is the visual "open chevron" shape and earns its number by popcount and symmetry class.

No other 3×3 GoL still-life exists. The next ones (loaf, pond, beehive) require more space. However, the 5-cell glider — the most famous pattern in cellular automata — does fit in 3×3, and its canonical orbit representative (under the lexicographic rule, smallest g-value = 107) is assigned to the Euro-pack vowel ò. 15 · Coda

The Tessera was not designed top-down; it emerged. The combinatorics of D₄ acting on a 3×3 grid happens to deliver an orbit count near the size of a real-world alphabet; the orbit structure happens to suggest a natural assignment; the centre pixel happens to act simultaneously as a vowel-lengthener and a retroflex marker; the chiral orbits happen to give exactly two characters each, fitting the bracket pairs. Whether this is mathematics making something easy or a designer noticing what was already there, the result is a writing system whose 104 characters are all derivable from a single primitive (the 3×3 binary grid), all renderable by a single algorithm, and — when written by hand — all recognisable from very small sketches indeed.

The system now has a name: Tessera — the small square tile from which a mosaic is assembled, which is what every character here is.

What remains? The phonotactics, prosody and syntax are entirely undefined; the assignment of vowels and consonants encodes a sound-system but does not enforce one. And the calligraphic rules, while complete, would benefit from a counterpart describing the connecting strokes of cursive writing — joining glyph to glyph rather than treating each as a separate mark. Future work, as they say.
